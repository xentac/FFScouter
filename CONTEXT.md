## Glossary

### Claimable Target
(Dibs system, in design.) An enemy faction member in an active ranked war who is currently in hospital with a known exit time. The hospital exit timestamp is the anchor for the entire dibs lifecycle — nothing without a hospital timer is ever claimable (okay, traveling-but-not-hospitalized, and early-revived players are permanently free-for-all in v1). Players hospitalized **abroad** are still Claimable Targets; location does not affect claimability, only who can usefully act on the claim.

### Hit Calling
The [[FFScouter Backend]]'s existing `/api/v1/hit-calling/*` API (premium-gated, faction-scoped), **being replaced** by the dibs system — no known users, no migration. Essentially chat dibs with a database: any-target-any-time claims resolved by arrival-order FIFO queue with a creation-anchored TTL, fully name-transparent within the faction. Beware the term collision: a Hit Calling "claim" is a *queue entry with a position*; a dibs [[Claim Request]] is an unordered member of a sealed set. Its abuse modes (mass pre-claiming across 30 targets, repeat-claim queue stuffing, racing-rewarded arrival ordering) are the negative design brief for [[Allocation]]'s invariants — see the "Relationship to the existing Hit Calling API" section of `docs/design/dibs.md`.

### Natural Hospital Exit
(Dibs system, in design.) A [[Claimable Target]] leaving hospital because their timer ran out, as opposed to an **early exit** (revive or self-medication). Dibs protection only ever applies to natural exits: an early exit immediately voids any in-flight claim requests or held dibs on that target and makes them free-for-all — "they didn't naturally come out of the hospital; people will be able to hit them no matter what."

### Claim Request
(Dibs system, in design.) A player's expression of interest in a [[Claimable Target]] during that target's open request window. Requests are never blocked or rationed — anyone eligible may request any target at any time while its window is open, including several overlapping targets at once. A request confers nothing by itself; only [[Allocation]] turns one request into [[Dibs]]. Distinct from Dibs: many players may hold requests on a target, at most one holds Dibs.

### Allocation
(Dibs system, in design.) The act of turning exactly one [[Claim Request]] into [[Dibs]]. Contention-triggered, not deadline-triggered: it runs at the configured pre-exit lead time (e.g. exit−30s in a 2:00/1:30 configuration) **only if requests are pending then** — a target with no requests stays open, and the first late request wins by instant grant (the fairness machinery exists for contention; an unrequested target is by definition uncontended, and the residual first-past-the-post race between two late requesters is accepted as rare and bounded). Losing a request does not destroy it: the request pool survives until the target exits, so a Dibs Holder's release re-runs allocation over the surviving requesters, reverting to open-for-requests only when the pool is empty. Target state at any moment is exactly one of *unclaimed / claimed-by-X / free-for-all*, and every participant sees the same answer. **Invariant: all requests within a window are identical** — arrival order/time is never an allocator input; requesting at 1:59 vs 1:31 confers nothing. This is the property that kills the fast-fingers race chat dibs runs on.

The default allocator is a deterministic ranked comparison — first differing factor wins: (1) under the [[Soft Dibs Cap]] beats at/over it; (2) least-recently-won dibs wins, with never-won counting as oldest; (3) lower war score wins; (4) coin flip. Deliberately deterministic rather than a weighted lottery so every allocation is explainable in one sentence ("B won because you won more recently") — explainability is what kills disputes. Factor 2 is the anti-starvation mechanism: sustained contention degrades gracefully into round-robin among active requesters, and a one-round penalty is the *maximum* cost of having farmed uncontested targets off-peak. A recent-win-count factor was explicitly rejected because it freezes out off-peak farmers for the whole recency window when contention arrives — punishing exactly the activity a farming faction wants to encourage. Factor order is faction-configurable; this is the default.

### Claim Space
(Dibs system, in design.) The complete dibs state one faction maintains against its ranked-war opponent: open request windows, pending [[Claim Request]]s, held [[Dibs]], configuration. **Sealed cross-faction** — invisible to the enemy down to the fact of its existence, since a leaked holder/expiry feed is live operational intel; every read/write is scoped server-side to the caller's verified faction. **Sealed-bid intra-faction** — during an open window faction-mates see only the request *count*, never requester names; after [[Allocation]] the holder and the one-line justification are public to the faction, but losers stay unnamed (a runner-up surfaces only if a release hands them the target). This is the social completion of the all-requests-identical invariant: visible names would let hierarchy (deference to leaders, watching who loses) re-enter through the sidebar, and it keeps losing emotionally cheap.

The war is the unit of all claim-space state: it begins empty at war start (everyone "never won" — first contested allocations fall through to score, then coin flip), and dies at war end (open windows and held dibs void, the allocator ledger never carries forward). Cross-war memory is deliberately none — last war's farming neither helps nor hurts this war. Only faction configuration and [[Faction Admin Authority]] delegation persist war-to-war.

### Dibs
(Dibs system, in design.) The exclusive, unambiguous claim on a single [[Claimable Target]], held by exactly one player (the **Dibs Holder**) as the outcome of [[Allocation]]. Exists to serve hit *farming* in ranked wars — the contested resource is hospital-exit hits. Dibs expires a configurable duration (the **grace**, default 30s, typical range 10–60s) after the target's [[Natural Hospital Exit]], after which the target is free-for-all. The system is on by default at the faction level (disable available) and is naturally dormant outside ranked wars — there is nothing to claim. Holding multiple dibs simultaneously is possible but governed by the [[Soft Dibs Cap]]. A held dibs ends by whichever comes first: early-exit void, holder release, grace expiry, or observed re-hospitalization of the target — **without attribution**: the system deliberately cannot tell "holder hit them" from "poacher hit them" (that would need faction-level API access), and nothing in the core loop needs to. Dibs is advisory, not enforced — Torn lets anyone press attack; what the system contributes to a poaching dispute is the thing chat never could, an unambiguous record of who held the claim. A brief post-grace window where an already-re-hospitalized target reads "free-for-all" is accepted as harmless (a hospitalized target is unactionable; their next exit starts a fresh cycle).

### Soft Dibs Cap
(Dibs system, in design.) A faction-configurable count (default **1**) of simultaneously held [[Dibs]] at or above which a player loses [[Allocation]] to any competing requester below the cap — but still wins uncontested. Deliberately soft, never a hard block: hard-denying the only interested player just sends the target free-for-all where they'd hit them anyway, losing the visibility benefit. Traditional chat-dibs culture is "one dib per person"; the soft cap preserves that default while improving on its behavior when nobody else is around.

### Dibs Badge
(Dibs system, in design.) The per-row indicator on a [[War List]] status cell — riding the hospital countdown, since every dibs state transition is anchored to that timer; deliberately not a new column (no width budget in the [[War Box]]) and not the FF cell (whose color already means [[FF Color Scale]]). Hand-drawn SVG glyphs per the [[Source Marker]] no-emoji rationale; no state is encoded by color alone. States: **no badge** when the machinery isn't engaged (window not yet open, or free-for-all — a naturally-exited target reads Okay with no badge, which is exactly "normal Torn rules apply"); **outline raised-hand + request count** (blue) when the window is open; the **hand fills** once you've requested; **filled crosshair, `You`** (green) plus a row *outline* (never a background fill — row backgrounds already carry other meanings) when you hold the dibs; **filled padlock** (gray, no name — width) when someone else does. Tap acts per state: request, withdraw, release; tapping another's padlock shows a transient "held by X" — who-holds-it is never more than a tap away, but never costs standing width. The badge is the only thing that persists on a row through the post-exit grace.

### Dibs Lifecycle Message
(Dibs system, in design.) The Discord bot's unit of output: one message per claimable window in a faction-configured channel, posted at window-open and edited in place through the claim lifecycle (request count → claimed-by → release/expiry/free), with state-contextual buttons (Request/Withdraw while open; Release visible only to the holder). Button replies are ephemeral, preserving the [[Claim Space]] sealed-bid property — nobody sees who requested. The resolved message is the faction's audit line — the channel history is the dispute record — subject to a configurable auto-cleanup delay after the target leaves hospital. Invariant: this message and the [[Dibs Badge]] are two renderings of one claim record, never two systems — a request from either surface updates both.

### Eligible Requester
(Dibs system, in design.) A current member of a faction in an active ranked war, with a **verified Torn player ID**, requesting targets in that war's opposing faction. Verification comes from either a validated API key on ffscouter.com (script users) or Torn's official Discord verification (torn.com/discord) for Discord-bot users — a bare Discord identity with neither is never enough, because [[Allocation]] ranks on per-player war data. Participation (request/withdraw/release) additionally requires **premium, hung on the Torn player ID rather than any API key** — which is what keeps keyless Discord users viable; in practice the adoption unit is a faction buying premium for all members. Reads (badge, counts, holder) are open to every verified faction member regardless of premium — a member who can't see "held by X" is a poaching accident waiting to happen — an asymmetry that is deliberately a raw deal for the individual non-premium member in a mixed faction. Outsiders (allies, mercs, alts outside the faction) are categorically ineligible. Mid-war membership churn is not a handled state: Torn locks faction membership during a ranked war. The system is symmetric — both factions in a war may independently run dibs against each other, in two disjoint claim spaces that never interact.

### Faction Admin Authority
An authorization concept that already exists on the [[FFScouter Backend]]: certain members hold faction-level administrative rights on ffscouter.com. The dibs system does not invent its own admin model — its faction configuration (window/allocation/grace timings, [[Soft Dibs Cap]], allocator factor order, enablement) is gated by this existing authority, identically on both surfaces (script UI and Discord bot). Mid-war config changes are prospective only: windows already open and dibs already held finish under the rules they started with.

### FFScouter Backend
The service at `ffscouter.com/api/v1` (`FF_SCOUTER_BASE_URL` in `src/utils/api.ts`) that this userscript queries for Fair Fight / Battle Stat estimates, sending the user's Torn API key as a URL query param on every call (`get-stats`, `check-key`, `player-flights`, `get-targets`). Operated as a partnership between Glasnost and xentac — neither party solely controls it, so user-facing docs (e.g. the GreasyFork additional-info doc) should link to its own published data policy (`https://ffscouter.com/#data-policy`) rather than asserting first-party claims about server-side retention/sharing on xentac's authority alone. Distinct from the "volunteer data gathering service," an unrelated, separate effort that this userscript does not interact with and that the linked data policy explicitly does not cover.


### Content Wrapper Width
The width budget our injected UI must lay out within, derived from Torn's `.content-wrapper` (the outer page container the [[Settings Panel]] attaches beside). Torn drives it from two viewport thresholds, giving three discrete widths:
- viewport ≥ **784px** → 784px (desktop)
- **386px** ≤ viewport < 784px → 386px (the "600px" figure sometimes quoted is inside this same bucket, not a separate threshold)
- viewport < **386px** → 320px (narrowest)
Only **784px** becomes an actual CSS breakpoint in our code (matching the existing `@media (min-width: 784px)` used by `.ff-filter-grid`); 386px and 320px both fall in the single "narrow" bucket where our grids collapse to one column. These are reference facts about Torn's layout, not values we set.

### Page React
`react`/`react-dom/client` are never bundled into the shipped `.user.js`. The build aliases them (`vite.config.ts`) to shims (`src/shims/react.ts`, `src/shims/react-dom.ts`, via the shared `src/shims/react-loader.ts`) that prefer `unsafeWindow.React`/`.ReactDOM` at call time, borrowing the copy Torn's own page already loads — a standalone UMD build served from `torn.com/builds/react-umd/...` that Torn's own app bundle also depends on via externals, so this isn't an incidental assumption. Saves ~500KB+ of shipped code, but depends on the userscript manager's cross-realm object bridging actually working; unreliable on at least one Safari/Tampermonkey configuration where `unsafeWindow` resolves to a genuinely separate realm that doesn't carry live page-realm object references (DOM access itself stays reliable there — only bridging to non-DOM JS objects is broken). Falls back to the same-realm copy set up by `@require`ing that exact Torn URL (`vite.config.ts`), not by fetching and `eval`-ing it ourselves — see ADR 0007 for why the eval approach failed on both Chrome (inconsistent CSP `unsafe-eval` grant) and Safari (evaluated code's `self`/`globalThis`/`window` resolution landed outside the observable realm) once actually tested, and why bundling React or reshaping the script's execution context were rejected too.

### Settings Panel
The `ff-settings-panel` custom element (`src/ui/settings-panel.tsx`) injected beside `.profile-wrapper` on profile pages — a plain `HTMLElement` subclass that mounts a React tree into itself via [[Page React]] rather than a LitElement. Organized into four [[Settings Group]]s (API Key & Premium, [[Gauge Marker Settings]], Feature Toggles, Debug Settings), each laid out as its own per-section CSS grid that reflows safely within the [[Content Wrapper Width]]: plain selects and checkboxes are single grid cells (3-up at ≥784px, 1-up when narrow), while multi-control bundles (API key row, FF ranges, marker size + preview, color scheme + swatches, chain sub-options, prose notices) span the full grid width as their own rows. Selects stack their label above a cell-filling control; checkboxes stay inline control-first; nothing overflows the container at any of the three widths. The footer buttons (Save/Reset/Clear Cache) sit outside any group, after the last one. Mounts on any profile page by default; `ffconfig.settingsPanelOwnProfileOnly` (a Feature Toggles checkbox, default off) restricts it to the logged-in user's own profile — see ADR 0008 for the detection mechanism and its asymmetric fail-open/fail-closed handling.

### Settings Group
`.ff-settings-group` — the visually distinct bordered card (background via `--ffscouter-alt-bg-color`, border via `--ffscouter-border-color`) wrapping each top-level section of the [[Settings Panel]], with an `<h4>` title inside the card instead of a bare heading. Written generically (not scoped to any one section's name) precisely because it was rolled out incrementally to every section in turn — [[Gauge Marker Settings]] first, then Feature Toggles, Debug Settings, and API Key & Premium — rather than being designed for a single consumer. There is no longer an ungrouped section in the panel.

### War Box
The `.faction-war` element containing the side-by-side enemy/your faction lists on the Ranked War page.

### War List
Either `.enemy-faction` or `.your-faction` — the individual per-faction member lists inside the [[War Box]]. Each has its own `.white-grad` header row and receives its own injected [[FF Column Header]].

### White-Grad Header
The `.white-grad` element inside a [[War List]] containing the native Torn column header divs (Member, Level, Score, Status, Attack). Our [[FF Column Header]] is injected as a sibling inside this element.

### FF Column Header
The `<div class="ffscouter-header">` injected by `apply_ff_columns()` into the [[White-Grad Header]]. Displays "FF" or "Est" depending on the configured column display mode. Does not use Torn's CSS module classes.

### Filter Box
The `FFFactionFilterBox` React component (`src/ui/faction-filter-box.tsx`), mounted into a plain container div via [[Page React]] (not a LitElement/custom element), that renders sort and filter controls. In war mode, a single shared instance sits at the top of the [[War Box]] and drives both [[War List]]s. Holds `sortBy` as its source of truth for sort state. Can be disabled per mode from the Settings panel (`faction_filter_enabled` / `war_filter_enabled`, default on); disabled means mounted-but-hidden with every snapshot forcing `filterEnabled: false` — not unmounted, since both FF/Est sort triggers need the live handle, and stored filter state is left untouched (see `docs/adr/0009-filter-box-disable-hidden-mount.md`).

### Filter Box Remount Leak
Since [[Filter Box]] is mounted via a plain `createRoot(container).render(...)` call in `mountFilterBox` rather than a custom element with `connectedCallback`/`disconnectedCallback` (contrast [[Settings Panel]]), nothing tears the previous mount down unless something explicitly calls `.unmount()`. Torn's faction/war tabs are hash-routed (`#/tab=info`, `#/war/...`), and `process_page()` re-runs on every hash change, calling `mountFilterBox` again against a brand-new `.members-list`/`.faction-war` element each time, with no reference kept to the previous mount. The retainer chain that keeps an abandoned mount alive: `window` (permanent) → its `resize`/`ff-config-updated` listeners (`faction-filter-box.tsx:398-399`, removed only by the mount effect's cleanup, which only runs on `.unmount()`) → the `onFilterChangeRef` closure → the old `membersList`/`factionWar` element captured by the `onFilterChange` callback at the original call site — pinning the entire previous member/war list DOM subtree in memory, once per tab switch, indefinitely. Fixed by tracking the current `Root` per mode inside `mountFilterBox` and unmounting the previous one before creating the next — see ADR 0010.

### Sort State
`"ff-asc" | "ff-desc" | "none"` — the current sort direction for the FF/Est column. Owned by the [[Filter Box]] and persisted to config. `"none"` means no FF sort is active (Torn's native sort is uninterrupted).

### Filter Collapse State
Whether the [[Filter Box]]'s `<details>` is collapsed, persisted per mode to config (`faction_filter_collapsed` / `war_filter_collapsed`). Owned by the [[Filter Box]] and seeded synchronously from config in the `useState` initializer so the first render already matches — correcting it in a mount effect instead forces an `open → closed` flip whose real-browser `toggle` event races `onToggle`'s write-back (see `docs/adr/0004-filter-collapse-sync-init.md`).

### Torn Sort Classes
Torn's CSS-module-hashed class names controlling the native column sort icon: `sortIcon___*` (the icon container), `desc___*` / `asc___*` (direction), `activeIcon___*` (currently active sort). These hashes change on Torn releases and should never be hardcoded.

### Sort Icon Class Detection
`detect_sort_icon_classes()` must be scoped to the list element being updated, not the full document. Both a [[War List]] and a standard member list can be visible simultaneously, each using a different CSS-module hash set for sort icons. A document-wide `querySelector` returns whichever set appears first in the DOM, applying the wrong classes to the other list. Fix: accept a `root: HTMLElement` parameter and query within that root.

### Column Display Mode
Resolved per call site (`apply_ff_columns`) from either `ffconfig.war_col_display` or `ffconfig.factions_col_display` depending on context, then passed into `apply_filters_and_sort` as part of the `filters` argument. Not re-read from config inside `apply_filters_and_sort` — doing so was both a localStorage hot-path hit and a correctness bug (war lists were always reading the faction display mode).

### TornTools Extra Rows
`.tt-last-action` and `.tt-stats-estimate` elements injected by TornTools after individual player rows, including on war page `.enemy` and `.your` rows. The sort path in `apply_filters_and_sort` collects and re-attaches these after each player row when reordering. The while loop that collects them must stop at `.table-row` (standard lists), `.enemy`, **and** `.your` (war lists) — a duplicate `"enemy"` check that omits `"your"` is a live bug causing all TornTools extras on the your-faction list to be collected by the first row. Distinct from [[TWSE Last Action Data]], which attaches directly to the row element rather than injecting siblings.

### Torn Clock
Torn exposes a server-synced clock via `window.getCurrentTimestamp()` on its own pages, distinct from the browser's local clock (`Date.now()`/`new Date()`), which can drift if the user's system clock is wrong. We encode this as two separate type families in `src/utils/types.ts`: `Timestamp`/`TimestampSec` (browser-clock-relative) vs `TornTimestamp`/`TornTimestampSec` (Torn-clock-relative). A value typed `TornTimestamp*` must have its age computed against the Torn Clock, not `Date.now()` — falling back to `Date.now()` only when `window.getCurrentTimestamp` isn't present (e.g. before Torn's own script has run on the page). The shared accessor lives in `src/utils/time.ts`. [[TWSE Last Action Data]] is a `TornTimestampSec` for this reason.

### TWSE Last Action Data
`data-twse-last-action-timestamp` — a `TornTimestampSec` (see [[Torn Clock]]) attribute published directly on war-page rows (`.enemy`/`.your`) by Torn War Stuff Enhanced (TWSE), a third-party tool, representing a player's last action time. Despite the name, TWSE only annotates [[War List]] rows — never the standard faction member list. Unlike [[TornTools Extra Rows]] (injected as sibling elements *after* a row), TWSE attaches its data directly to the row element itself, closer in shape to FFScouter's own `data-earliest-arrival`/`data-latest-arrival` attributes (`column-population.ts`) than to TornTools' sibling-row approach. A value of `0` is TWSE's sentinel for "last action unknown" (it still sets the attribute, just with this value) rather than omitting the attribute — treated as no-data, not as an actual 1970 timestamp, by both [[TWSE Presence Detection]] and the [[Last Action Range Filter]].

### TWSE Presence Detection
Whether TWSE is active on a war page is determined by scanning both [[War List]]s for at least one row carrying [[TWSE Last Action Data]] — not all rows, since TWSE may legitimately skip a row it can't resolve. Pushed onto `<ff-faction-filter-box>` as a property in `setup_reapply_watcher`, the same "setup code detects page context, then configures the filter box" shape already used for `mode` (see [[Filter Box]]). TWSE runs as an independent script and may annotate rows after our watcher is already set up, so detection re-runs from three places: once synchronously at setup, again whenever `data-twse-last-action-timestamp` changes on a row (added to the existing attribute `MutationObserver`'s filter — safe to share with that observer, since `apply_filters_and_sort`'s own row reordering only ever performs `childList` mutations, never attribute changes, so it can't retrigger this attributes-only watch), and on the same 30s interval already used to poll traveling flights, as a fallback for TWSE adding the attribute via a brand-new row element rather than annotating an existing one (a `childList` mutation the attribute observer doesn't watch). If the attribute later disappears (TWSE disabled, navigated away and back), the [[Last Action Range Filter]] group hides again, but its stored Min/Max values are left untouched in config — they reappear pre-filled if TWSE returns.

### Last Action Range Filter
A Min/Max range filter (`Min ≤ age ≤ Max`, the same direction as the Level/FF/Stats ranges) on a player's age since last action, computed against the [[Torn Clock]] from [[TWSE Last Action Data]]. Only ever rendered in war mode, gated by [[TWSE Presence Detection]] — faction mode never shows it. Min/Max are stored as raw typed duration strings (e.g. `"1h"`, `"4h2m15s"`) and parsed to seconds only when the filter snapshot is built, the same deferred-parsing convention the Stats range filter uses for its suffix-number strings (`parse_suffix_number` in `src/utils/strings.ts`) — parsing eagerly on every keystroke would make the input glitch mid-type. Unparseable text resolves to "no bound" rather than hiding every row. A row whose own last-action value is the `0` sentinel (see [[TWSE Last Action Data]]) always passes this filter, the same graceful fallback already used for rows missing FF/Stats data.

### Active-Filter Flag
`data-ffscouter-active-filter`, set by `is_filter_active()` in `filter-sort-engine.ts`. Despite the generic-sounding name, this means specifically "FFScouter is currently sorting by FF/Est" — it does not mean "any filter is active," and deliberately ignores the Level/FF/Stats/[[Last Action Range Filter]] bounds. Do not "fix" this to account for range filters; the narrow meaning is intentional.

### Torn Row Reordering
Torn's own JavaScript reorders faction member rows in response to the same state-change events (status, activity) that trigger our `MutationObserver`. Because of this, `apply_filters_and_sort` must always re-sort when called from the mutation path — not just reapply filters — to reassert our sort order over Torn's.

### Cleanup Interval
A `setInterval` (10 s period) attached to each active observer setup that checks `!element.isConnected` and tears down its associated `MutationObserver` and flight-poll interval when the element leaves the DOM. Preferred over a document-level `childList`+`subtree` `MutationObserver` because the Torn SPA produces many DOM mutations per second and a subtree observer would impose constant allocation and callback overhead.

### FF Color Scale
The single FF-value-to-hex-color mapping (`get_ff_arrow_colour` / `get_ff_colour` in `src/utils/strings.ts`) applied uniformly across every place an FF/BS value is color-coded: both [[Gauge Marker]] variants (arrow and bubble), the profile mini [[Info Line]] background, and the faction/war page FF column cell background. One scale, one preset choice — never configured per-consumer. See [[Color Scheme]] for how the scale's 11 colors are chosen, and ADR 0002 for why it stays a single shared scale.

### Color Scheme
`ffconfig.color_scheme` — selects which 11-hex-color palette the [[FF Color Scale]] indexes into. Eight named presets ship in the settings panel (`classic` is the default and matches the scale's original hardcoded colors exactly): `classic`, `cool_diverging`, `neon`, `colorblind_safe`, `grayscale`, `green_yellow_red`, `blue_yellow_red`, `plasma`. The last three (plus `colorblind_safe`, which matches Grafana's `Viridis`) are modeled on Grafana's default field color schemes for fidelity rather than invented from scratch. A ninth value, `custom`, is reserved in the type/storage layer (`ffconfig.custom_colors: string[] | null`, 11 hex strings) but intentionally hidden from the settings dropdown until a color-editing UI exists. The no-data indicator color (black) does not vary by scheme — it isn't a point on the gradient.

### Gauge Marker
The visual indicator placed on a `.ffscouter-gauge` track (see `make_marker()` in `src/utils/dom.ts`) showing a player's FF/BS value at the position computed by `ff_to_percent`. Style is selected via `ffconfig.gauge_marker_type`: `arrow` (the default colored arrow, `.ffscouter-arrow`) or one of two bubble variants (`bubble_ff`, `bubble_estimate`, rendered as `.ffscouter-bubble`). Its size is controlled by [[Marker Scale]], independent of which style is chosen. `make_marker()` returns the arrow/bubble wrapped in a `.ffscouter-marker-wrapper` div, which is what actually carries the absolute gauge-track positioning (previously on `.ffscouter-arrow`/`.ffscouter-bubble` directly) — the wrapper exists so the optional [[Source Marker]] badge has a single containing block to anchor to regardless of which shape is active. `.ffscouter-gauge` is applied indiscriminately to whatever host element a feature attaches to — see [[Gauge Attach Mode]] for why that host isn't always shaped like an actual gauge track.

### Gauge Attach Mode
`GaugeAttachMode` (`HONOR_BAR` | `FALLBACK`), an explicit parameter threaded through `add_ff_arrow`/`apply_ff_gauge`/`apply_ff_gauge_selector` (`src/utils/dom.ts`) and stamped as `data-ffscouter-attach-mode` on the host element alongside `.ffscouter-gauge`. Exists because the element a [[Gauge Marker]] attaches to is not one shape: `HONOR_BAR` means the host is `.honor-text-wrap`, an actual bar graphic the marker rides as a needle (a ~14-19px tall element the marker hovers above via a fixed cosmetic offset). `FALLBACK` covers every other host a feature attaches to when a player has honor bars disabled — `.member`, `.user.name`, `[class*="userInfoBlock__"]`, bazaar card variants, forum poster classes, etc. — none of which are bars, and none of which share a consistent height, even across rows using the same selector. Deliberately an explicit parameter rather than inferred from the host's own classes (e.g. `:not(.honor-text-wrap)`): the fallback set has no natural boundary, so a future third shape (neither a real bar nor "just center it") has somewhere to go. Only affects *vertical* positioning of the [[Gauge Marker]] — the horizontal gauge-track math is unaffected, since fallback targets are deliberately chosen (by whoever adds a new one) to have a consistent width per selector, even where height isn't guaranteed. See ADR 0006. Mini Profile always has an honor bar and never uses `FALLBACK`; Item Market's own page-specific selectors (bazaar cards, `userInfoWrapper__`) are `FALLBACK` despite living in their own feature rather than being dispatched through the `fallback` feature's shared selector list.

### Marker Scale
`ffconfig.gauge_marker_scale` — a percentage (50–200, step 5; default 100) that uniformly resizes both the arrow and bubble [[Gauge Marker]] variants. Implemented as a single shared CSS custom property, `--ffscouter-marker-scale`, set on `document.body`: `--ffscouter-arrow-width` and `--ffscouter-bubble-font-size` are both `calc()`'d off it, so the arrow's width (and, via its native SVG aspect ratio, its height) and the bubble's font-size scale together from one value. The bubble's box (height, min-width, line-height, padding) is defined in `em` so it scales automatically off the font-size; `border-radius` is a large constant (`999px`) so it always renders as a full pill regardless of size. Only the bubble's own pill shape scales this way — the underlying `.ffscouter-gauge` track is untouched.

Unlike [[Color Scheme]] and Gauge Marker Style — which are baked into each marker inline at creation time and only affect markers created after the change — Marker Scale is live: because every marker reads the same body-level CSS variable, changing it (in `add_ff_arrow()` at creation time, and again explicitly on Settings save) instantly resizes every marker already on the page, with no need to recreate elements.

### Marker Border
The outline drawn around a [[Gauge Marker]] — the arrow's SVG `stroke` and the bubble's CSS `border`, both currently hardcoded to a fixed black/near-black width regardless of [[Marker Scale]]. Distinct from the `.ffscouter-gauge` track, which has no border of its own — "the border around the gauge" colloquially refers to this marker outline, not the track. Thickness is becoming user-configurable (one setting driving both the arrow and bubble variants), scaling proportionally with Marker Scale the same way the arrow's outline already does today. Like [[Color Scheme]] and Gauge Marker Style (and unlike Marker Scale itself), it is baked into each marker at creation time — changing it doesn't affect markers already on the page until they're recreated.

### Gauge Marker Settings
The [[Settings Group]] consolidating every setting that configures the [[Gauge Marker]]: marker style (arrow/bubble), [[Marker Scale]], [[Color Scheme]], [[Marker Border]] thickness, and the FF Ranges (Low/High/Max) that position the marker on the gauge track via `ff_to_percent`. Deliberately includes FF Ranges even though it's a data threshold rather than a visual setting — the group is "everything that affects the gauge," not strictly "everything visual." Placed directly beneath the API Key & Premium group and above Feature Toggles; this was the first section to get the [[Settings Group]] card treatment, before it was rolled out to the rest of the panel.

In the settings panel, configured via a range slider paired with a synced, clamped numeric input, plus a live preview showing a sample arrow and bubble side by side (colored from a mid-range entry of the currently selected draft [[Color Scheme]]) that reacts to the unsaved draft value via a locally-scoped `--ffscouter-marker-scale` override on the preview wrapper — independent of the saved/body-level value used by real markers on the page.

### Info Line
The `ff-header-line` React component (`src/ui/info-line.tsx`), mounted via [[Page React]] (not a LitElement/custom element), rendering the FF/BS summary line on profile pages, including a background colored via the [[FF Color Scale]].

### Activity Status
Online/idle/offline state derived per-row from the `aria-label` ("{name} is online/offline/idle") on Torn's `userStatusWrap___*` div (`get_activity_status()` in `src/utils/dom.ts`), not from an `<img alt>` as in pre-2026-06 markup — `.icons` is now reused for multiple unrelated row sections (e.g. the achievement-icon tray on the regular faction member list) and can no longer scope the lookup. An unparseable label yields `"unknown"`, which always passes the [[Filter Box]]'s activity filter rather than being hidden, and is logged once per distinct label text so a future wording change is loud, not silent. See [[Torn Markup Snapshot]].

### Torn Markup Snapshot
A dated, untrimmed capture of a live Torn page fragment, stored under a feature's `__fixtures__/torn-markup/<date>/` directory (e.g. `src/features/faction/__fixtures__/torn-markup/2026-06-22/`). Captured opportunistically when Torn changes markup; the latest dated folder is wired into a test asserting our parsing still produces the expected result, so the snapshot and the parser can't silently drift apart. Older dated folders are kept for reference but not re-tested. Distinct from the trimmed inline `innerHTML` strings used elsewhere in `.test.ts` files, which exist only to exercise one assertion and aren't meant to be faithful.

### Estimate Source
`"bss" | "premium" | "spies"` (`EstimateSource` in `src/utils/types.ts`) — the three sources the get-stats endpoint can merge an estimate from, matching both its top-level `source` field and the keys of [[Available Estimates]]. `"bss"` is public battle-stat data, `"premium"` is premium spy data, `"spies"` is a complete faction spy record.

### Available Estimates
`available_estimates` on `FFDataComplete` (`src/utils/types.ts`) — the get-stats endpoint's three per-[[Estimate Source]] estimate candidates, returned alongside its own merged top-level fields, each independently nullable when that source had nothing to offer. Cached verbatim rather than collapsed to a single winner, specifically so a later priority policy (e.g. "no spy older than 30 days," "prefer premium over standard") can re-rank already-cached data without a refetch. See [[Estimate Resolution]] for how a winner is currently picked.

### Estimate Resolution
The single seam (`resolve_estimate` in `src/utils/estimate.ts`) where an [[Estimate Source]] priority policy would live once one exists. Today it's a no-op: it always picks `available_estimates[data.source]`, i.e. whatever FFScouter's own merge already chose. Every UI consumer reads estimate fields through the thin `extract_ff` / `extract_bs_estimate` / `extract_bs_estimate_human` / `extract_source` / `extract_last_updated` accessors built on top of it, rather than touching `fair_fight`/`bs_estimate`/etc. on `FFDataComplete` directly — so a future priority config only has to change `resolve_estimate`, not every call site. Deliberately plain functions over plain data, not a class: IndexedDB (`ffcache.ts`) round-trips `FFDataComplete` through the structured clone algorithm, which drops class prototypes, so a class-based wrapper would silently lose its methods the first time a record came back out of the cache (see ADR 0005). Falls back field-by-field to the top-level `FFDataComplete` values (never to a different [[Available Estimates]] entry) whenever the chosen candidate is missing or has a null field for that field — the same "always give the user something" rule applied throughout.

### Spy Snapshot
One entry in the `spies` array on `FFDataComplete`/get-stats (`src/utils/types.ts`) — up to three raw individual faction spy reports (newest first), each with its own stat block, `last_updated`, and `source_faction_id`. Distinct from [[Available Estimates]]' `spies` candidate, which is FFScouter's own single chosen best spy value, not the raw list. Selecting a specific snapshot from this array (rather than trusting FFScouter's own pick) is an explicit non-goal for now.

### Source Marker
A small icon shown next to an estimate indicating which [[Estimate Source]] produced it: a magnifying glass for `spies`, a gold 5-point star for `premium` (a pentagon "gem" shape was tried first but read as an unrecognizable blob rather than anything premium-related), and deliberately no icon for `bss` — absence-of-marker-means-ordinary, the same convention `no_data` vs. normal data already uses. `get_source_marker` in `src/utils/strings.ts` maps a source to a `{ icon: "spy" | "premium", label }` descriptor; the actual shapes are hand-drawn SVGs (outline stroke + solid fill, the same visual language as the FF arrow marker) built from geometry/color constants also in `strings.ts` (`SOURCE_MARKER_VIEWBOX`, `SPY_ICON_LENS`/`SPY_ICON_HANDLE`/`SPY_ICON_COLOR`, `PREMIUM_ICON_PATH_D`/`PREMIUM_ICON_COLOR`). Deliberately not emoji: emoji color/shape depends on the OS/browser emoji font, which made an earlier emoji-based version read as visually "busy" and clash unpredictably with whatever Torn page background it sat over — a fixed solid fill sidesteps both. Two independent renderers consume those shared constants: `make_source_marker_svg` in `src/utils/dom.ts` (raw DOM, via `innerHTML`) for the [[Gauge Marker]] and Mini Profile, and the `SourceMarkerIcon` React component (`src/ui/source-marker-icon.tsx`) for [[Info Line]] and the Settings Panel preview — a raw `SVGElement` can't be dropped into JSX, so each side builds its own markup, the same split `FF_ARROW_PATH_D` already has between `make_arrow` and the JSX preview arrow. Every icon carries an SVG `<title>` plus `role="img"`/`aria-label` so desktop hover and assistive tech get the full label; the icon itself (not the tooltip) is what's visible without hovering, since this runs on phones where hover doesn't exist. Shown on the [[Info Line]] and Mini Profile (right after the FF value, sized to the surrounding text via `.ffscouter-inline-source-marker`) and on the [[Gauge Marker]] (floating over the marker's own top-right corner via `.ffscouter-marker-wrapper`, sized by `--ffscouter-source-marker-size`, scaling with [[Marker Scale]] the same way [[Marker Border]] does). Deliberately absent from the faction/war list columns.
